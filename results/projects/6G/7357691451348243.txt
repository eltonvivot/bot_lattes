2012 - Atual
Structure property correlation in luminescent rare earth doped glasses and ceramics
Descrição: The development of new solid state laser hosts for high optical quality rare-earth ion doped crystals, glasses, and ceramics is an active area of current materials research. To facilitate the design of materials with optimized emission properties, detailed structural information at the atomic level is essential, regarding the local bonding environment (siting) of the rare earth ions, their second-nearest coordination spheres, and their extent of local clustering, as well as the distribution over separate micro- or nanophases in composite materials. In this project, solid state nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) and electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) will be used to seek a fundamental understanding of the spectroscopic and photophysical properties of such material systems on a structural basis. The systems to be studied are rare earth containing aluminophosphate, oxyfluoride and fluorophosphate glasses, with systematic variation of host composition, as well as ion species and concentration. In addition sol-gel derived guest-host systems will be examined. Because of their inherent atomic paramagnetism the fluorescent rare-earth species themselves are not accessible to solid state NMR investigations. Thus the NMR strategy for obtaining structural information is based on a three-fold approach: 1) the NMR spectra of the probe nuclei 45Sc and 89Y associated with diamagnetic ?mimics? of the fluorescent species will be analyzed, 2) the effect of paramagnetic doping on the NMR signals of constituent nuclei in the glass or ceramic framework will be examined, and 3) pulsed EPR techniques will be used to characterize the electron spin echo envelope modulation produced by the interactions of the rare-earth ions with the surrounding nuclear magnetic moments. The structural information obtained by these approaches and its correlation with the photophysical properties of these materials will be important for developing new design strategies for high power laser systems and other o.
